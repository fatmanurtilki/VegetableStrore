The fig is recognized as one of the world's oldest cultivated fruits, with its history reaching back approximately 5,000 years to the Near East, specifically the region of Upper Mesopotamia. From its origins, it spread throughout the Mediterranean Basin, becoming a staple in ancient civilizations like Greece and Rome. Due to its significance, the fig quickly adopted symbolic meaning, representing fertility, abundance, and prosperity across cultures. The Greek philosopher Plato famously called the fig the "Friend of Philosophers" due to the belief that it strengthened the mind. Furthermore, the fruit holds deep religious importance, frequently mentioned in the sacred texts of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. While it initially thrived in Western Asia, its cultivation expanded westward, embedding the fig in the agriculture and mythology of the entire Mediterranean world. Today, it remains a globally popular fruit, consumed both fresh and dried.

The peach boasts a history that spans millennia, predating human cultivation in some forms. Its definitive origin is traced back to East Asia and Central China, where it was first domesticated around 2000 BCE. In Chinese mythology and culture, the peach quickly gained immense significance as a potent symbol of immortality, longevity, and spring. Although its botanical name, persica, led many early observers to incorrectly assume its origin was Persia (Iran), the fruit was primarily transported westward through that region. The peach made its way into the Western world via the Romans around the 1st century, who spread it throughout Europe. Later, in the 16th century, Spanish explorers introduced the peach to the Americas, where it has since become a major crop. Today, the peach is a globally cherished fruit, with significant production across Europe, the Americas, and beyond, continuing its legacy as a cultural icon and culinary delight.
